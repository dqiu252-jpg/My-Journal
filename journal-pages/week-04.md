## Activity 1
Using Ollama was a different experience compared to cloud-based AI tools. I downloaded the model and ran it locally, which meant everything stayed on my computer. This made me feel more in control of my data, as nothing was being sent online. The responses were quite fast, but I noticed that the quality was sometimes less detailed compared to tools like ChatGPT. When I asked it to generate ideas for visualising my phone data or sound data, it gave simple and direct suggestions, but they were not as creative or refined.

I also tried asking it to write p5.js code. It worked for basic sketches, but sometimes the code needed fixing or was missing details. This showed me the limitations of smaller models. However, it was still useful for quick ideas and experimentation. I think the main tradeoff is between privacy and capability. Ollama gives more control and independence, but cloud AI is more powerful and flexible. This activity helped me understand how different AI tools can support design in different ways.
## Activity 2
Using NotebookLM felt more advanced and connected to my own work. I added my Making Journal, design references, and previous experiments, which allowed the AI to understand my interests and process. Writing the context.md file helped guide the AI and made the responses more focused.

When I used the chat, I found it interesting how the AI could analyse my work and suggest what I care about. It identified themes like personal data, human experience, and creative coding, which I didn’t clearly realise before. It also generated new ideas and provocations, which helped me think more critically about my work. Compared to Ollama, the responses were more detailed and insightful.

The Audio Overview was a unique experience. Hearing my work being discussed felt different from reading text. It made my project feel more real and helped me notice patterns and connections more clearly. However, some parts were slightly inaccurate or generalised, which shows that AI interpretation is not always perfect.

## Independent Study
### Step 1
I chose the dataset River water quality trends, 2008–2017, 1998–2017, and 1990–2017 because it reflects environmental conditions in Aotearoa New Zealand over time. I was interested in how river health changes across different periods and what this might reveal about human impact, pollution, and environmental management. This dataset is meaningful because rivers are an important part of everyday life, ecology, and cultural identity in Aotearoa, especially for Māori communities.

![alt text](<../assets/week-04/week4 img1.png>)

### Step 2
I uploaded the CSV file into an AI tool and asked it to explain the dataset. The data includes different time ranges (short-term, medium-term, and long-term trends) and measures changes in river water quality over time. The columns represent indicators such as nutrient levels, clarity, and overall trends (improving, worsening, or stable).

The AI helped identify patterns, such as whether water quality is improving or declining in certain periods. It also suggested possible stories, like how environmental policies or land use might affect river health. However, the AI did not fully explain the context of how the data was collected or the limitations of the dataset. I had to think critically about what might be missing, such as local differences or cultural perspectives.

#### Reflection

In the first version, a simple chart is used to present the data clearly. The focus is on accuracy and readability rather than design. A basic bar or line chart format is chosen because it is familiar and easy to understand. The visual encoding mainly relies on position and size to represent values, with very little use of colour. This version presents the raw data without interpretation, allowing viewers to see the values directly, but it remains technical and less engaging for a general audience.

In the second version, AI improves the visualisation by focusing on one variable, NO3N, and introducing colour to show differences in water quality. The visual encoding is changed by adding a colour system, where green, orange, and red represent low, medium, and high pollution levels. This makes the information easier to understand at a glance. The layout is also simplified by reducing unnecessary details and highlighting the most important aspects of the data. These changes make the visualisation more accessible and easier for a general audience to interpret.

In the third version, AI further improves the design by transforming the chart into a narrative infographic. The format is changed from a standard graph to a storytelling layout, using the question “Is it safe to swim?” to connect the data to a real-life context. Multiple visual elements are introduced, including colour, icons, and short explanatory text, to communicate meaning more clearly. Green represents improving water quality, red shows degrading conditions, and grey indicates insufficient data. The focus shifts from displaying raw data to summarising trends across different time periods. This makes the final version more engaging, informative, and suitable for a general audience.

### Step 4 - Critically Evaluate

The AI defaulted to standard charts like line graphs and bar charts, with simple layouts and generic titles. The visuals were clear but basic and not very engaging for a general audience.

I had to override this by directing the design towards storytelling and clearer communication. I introduced colour to show meaning, simplified the language, and changed the format into a question-based infographic to make it more relatable.

The AI assumed the data should be shown in a technical way and that the audience would understand variables like NO3N or E. coli. This made the initial outputs less accessible for non-experts.

The most interesting version is the final infographic because it communicates a clear message and connects the data to real life, making it easier and more engaging to understand.

If I worked without AI, I would explore more creative ideas earlier and focus on storytelling from the start, instead of beginning with standard charts.

### Documentation
D’Ignazio and Klein’s ideas raise questions about whose perspective is represented and what information is included or left out. In my work, this made me think about how simplifying data might hide complexity, and how design choices can influence interpretation.

Mikaere’s framing of data as a strategic asset for Māori development highlights the importance of data ownership and cultural context. It made me consider that this dataset is not neutral, and that data about land and water should be treated carefully and respectfully, especially in relation to Māori knowledge and values.

Working with AI as a design tool was helpful for generating quick ideas and starting points, but it required guidance to produce meaningful results. It showed me that AI supports the design process but does not replace critical thinking or creativity.

With more time, I would develop a more interactive version, explore additional variables, and refine the visual design further. I would also include more context to better explain the data and its impact.

Overall, this process showed me that design decisions strongly affect how data is understood, and that combining AI with human judgement leads to better outcomes.
