# Week 01



## Data Drawings-In Class activities

In week one's class we have a workshop on data drawing. We worked in a group of four and created some questions such as how long we slept last night, how we got to school, and how much we weigh. Then we collected the data from each group member. This activity was very interesting because everyone in the group is different. We have a variety of people with different backgrounds, including Chinese and Polynesian members. This made our data more diverse and meaningful, and it helped us understand each other better through simple personal information. 

Then we start data drawing by using the information we gathered earlier. In the drawing, we used simple icons like a bed, bus, car, and walking figure to show different answers. The lines and colours connect each person’s data, making it look like a journey or daily routine. I think this drawing is interesting because it shows both data and personality at the same time. It is not perfect or clean, but it feels more human. It also helped us understand each other better in a visual way.

## Independent Study - “When and why I check my phone” Data Portrait

I chose to track when and why I check my phone because it is something I do many times every day without really thinking about it. It is a small habit, but it happens often, and I was curious to understand it better. I wanted to see if there were patterns in my behaviour, such as checking my phone when I feel bored, stressed, or tired. This topic felt personal and relevant to my daily life, and it was something I could easily observe over several days.

Collecting the data by hand was an interesting experience. I carried a small notebook and tried to record each time I picked up my phone, including the time, my reason, how I felt, and how long I stayed on it. Sometimes I forgot to write it down, which showed me how automatic this habit is. Writing things by hand made me slow down and pay more attention to my actions in the moment. When I started to visualise the data, I found it more creative and expressive than using digital tools. I used colours, shapes, and positions to represent different feelings and behaviours, which made the data feel more personal.

Through this process, I noticed patterns that I would not have seen before. For example, I realised that I check my phone more often at night and when I feel bored or tired. I also noticed that many times I pick up my phone without a clear reason, just out of habit. This made me more aware of how often I use my phone and how it connects to my emotions.

In my data collection, I chose to focus on time, reason, feeling, and duration. This emphasises my emotional state and daily habits rather than exact details. I decided not to record specific apps or exact minutes because I wanted to keep the process simple and focus on the overall experience. This means some detailed information is left out, but it allows the data to feel more human and reflective.

This exercise connects to data humanism and the Dear Data project because it focuses on personal, everyday experiences. Instead of clean and perfect data, it includes imperfections, emotions, and subjectivity. The hand-drawn visualisation helps tell a story about my life, making the data more meaningful and relatable.

Overall, this activity helped me become more aware of my habits and showed me that data can be creative, personal, and expressive, not just technical.
