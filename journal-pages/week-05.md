## Proposal

Through the course experiments, I found myself most engaged with projects
that translated small, everyday experiences into visual or physical forms,
especially the phone tracking exercise and the hand-drawn data portrait.
These activities revealed patterns in my behaviors that I normally overlook,
such as moments of distraction, repetition, and unconscious habits. What I
found most meaningful was how simple data could become expressive when
it was drawn by hand or interpreted creatively. This led me to become
interested in ideas of data humanism and data physicalizing, where data is
not just something to analyze, but something that can be felt, experienced,
and reflected upon. Compared to digital outputs, which often feel precise and
controlled, my handmade work felt more open, imperfect, and connected to
lived experience. This contrast has shaped the direction of my project, as I
want to explore how data can remain human even when it is structured or
collected systematically.

For this project, I will focus on everyday habits and how they leave traces over
time, particularly moments of attention and distraction. These are small,
repeated actions that often go unnoticed, yet they form a large part of daily
life. I am interested in imagining a future scenario where these traces are
continuously collected and made visible, shaping how people understand
themselves. In this scenario, behaviors becomes a form of data that
accumulates and can be interpreted in different ways. The data for this project
will be self-collected over several days but intentionally kept simple and open.
Rather than aiming for accuracy or completeness, the focus will be on
capturing moments as they are experienced. This approach connects to ideas
of datafication and surveillance, as it reflects how everyday actions can be
turned into data. It also relates to data feminism by questioning how complex
human experiences are simplified into categories, and to Māori data
sovereignty by raising questions about who owns, controls, and interprets
personal data.

The outcome of this project will be a physical accumulation of data that
develops over time. This may take the form of a string, a sequence of small
objects, or a growing structure made from simple materials. Each data point
will be represented by a small physical element, with variations in colour, size,
or spacing used to suggest different qualities such as time, intensity, or
feeling. The exact system will remain flexible, allowing the process of making
to influence the final form. As more data is collected, the structure will grow,
creating patterns through repetition, density, and layering. This makes the
passage of time and accumulation visible in a way that is difficult to achieve
through digital visualization alone.

The provocation of this project is to make invisible behaviors visible through
accumulation, while still leaving space for interpretation. I want the audience
to notice how small, everyday actions can build into something larger and
more complex over time. Rather than presenting clear or fixed meanings, the
work invites viewers to reflect on their own habits and consider how their
actions might also be recorded and represented. By keeping the outcome
open-ended and material-based, the project encourages imagination and
personal interpretation, while remaining simple and achievable to produce and
present.
